## 1. [AMBER] arxiv-openproblem:1712.01960v1  (composite 4.9357, confidence medium)
**Source:** arxiv-openproblem · **Field:** ["discrete-geometry", "combinatorics", "metric-geometry", "optimization"] · **Flags:** quantitative_extension,construction_target,default_method_stalled · [https://arxiv.org/abs/1712.01960v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1712.01960v1)

**Problem:** Determine the worst-case minimal distortion required to embed an n-point diversity into l1 (the diversity analogue of the classical metric embedding into l1). In particular, give bounds or exact values for the function f(n) = max_{diversity on n points} (minimal distortion to embed that diversity into l1).

**Win condition:** Produce one or more explicit, machine-checkable certificates for small n (e.g. n ≤ 8–12): either (A) a diversity instance together with a proof/certificate (embedding map into l1 and computed distortion) showing a new higher lower bound on worst-case distortion for that n, or (B) an explicit embedding algorithm and concrete embeddings achieving a strictly better upper bound for some n. Certificates should be runnable checks (LP/ILP instances, explicit coordinate vectors, and computed distortion values), and ideally a formalized Lean statement verifying the distortion computation for at least one nontrivial n.

**Closest prior / novelty check:** Closest prior is not a solution of f(n), but it narrows the battlefield. Bryant–Tupper’s 2017 problem statement gives the general upper bound O(n) and only the inherited metric lower bound Ω(log n); it also records special classes: Steiner/TSP diversities O(log n), diameter/ball/partition-diversity combinations then O(log^2 n), and symmetric diversities constant distortion. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1712.01960)) The main later update I found is Jozefiak–Shepherd 2023: diameter diversities improve to randomized polynomial-time O(log n) distortion into l1; however they explicitly state that the smallest known distortion for an arbitrary diversity is still O(n). They also give an algorithmic hardness result: no polynomial-time embedding querying only sets of size O(log^p n) can achieve O(n^{1−ε}) distortion unless P=NP, which kills a large class of induced-metric/query-limited attacks but does not close the existential worst-case f(n). ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.04199)) Bryant–Tupper’s 2024/2026 “Linear and Sublinear Diversities” studies embeddability into linear/sublinear/Minkowski diversity classes and characterizes negative-type/generalized-circumradius cases; I found no claim that it resolves arbitrary finite l1 distortion. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.07092)) Bottom line: no Erdősgate kill found; the full arbitrary-diversity worst-case remains apparently open, but the literature is close enough that small-n certificates need to be framed as computational extremal data, not as solving the asymptotic problem.

**Residual (amber) risk:** The proposed win condition—explicit machine-checkable certificates for n≤8–12—looks plausibly novel because searches for exact small-n f(n), LP certificate tables, or GitHub code tied to arXiv:1712.01960 produced no relevant hits. The key caveat: n=3/4-type results may be regarded as too small unless they expose a new pattern, facet, or lower-bound family. Also beware that any l1 diversity is a nonnegative combination of split/cut diversities, a fact already noted by Bryant–Tupper, so a certificate consisting only of this LP formulation is not novel; novelty must be an explicit extremal δ, exact rational distortion, and a dual/Farkas certificate showing optimality. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1712.01960)) I would target: (1) exact f(3), f(4), maybe f(5) as sanity checks; (2) record lower-bound instances for n=6–12 with exact LP optima; (3) compare against the trivial O(n) embedding and the Ω(log n) inherited metric lower bound. If the best found examples are symmetric/diameter/Steiner, they are likely not publishable because those classes are already covered by constant/O(log n) results. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1604.01863))

**LLM-saturation:** I found no indexed evidence that this exact arXiv problem has been attacked in a frontier-lab benchmark or public formal-conjectures-style repository; searches for the title, arXiv id, GitHub mentions, AlphaEvolve, and diversity embeddings did not surface such a run. General LLM/evolution saturation risk is real but indirect: AlphaEvolve is a publicized LLM-guided evolutionary coding system for machine-gradeable scientific/mathematical discovery, and a later AlphaEvolve math report says it tested 67 problems across analysis, combinatorics, geometry, and number theory, often rediscovering or improving known constructions. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.13131?utm_source=openai)) This problem is highly machine-gradeable via LP dual certificates, so it is exactly the kind of finite extremal task such systems could attack, but I found no public sign they already did.

**Attack sketch:** Engine A — LP/duality/cut-cone route. For a fixed rational diversity δ on [n], represent an l1 diversity as δ̂(A)=Σ_S λ_S·1[A∩S≠∅ and A\S≠∅], over nontrivial splits S modulo complement, λ_S≥0. Minimize c subject to δ(A)≤δ̂(A)≤cδ(A) for every |A|≥2. This is a rational LP; the certificate is: δ table, primal split weights λ, optimal c, and a rational dual certificate proving no smaller c. For lower-bound search, mutate δ within the diversity cone: δ(A)≥0, δ(singletons)=0, and δ(A∪B)≤δ(A∪C)+δ(B∪C) for C≠∅. Use LP separation rather than enumerating all diversity inequalities when n grows. Engine A should also try quotients/symmetries: orbit-reduced LPs under transitive groups, hypergraph-Steiner diversities from small hypergraphs, and perturbations between diameter and Steiner extremes, since arbitrary δ sits between δ_diam and δ_S for its induced metric. Bryant–Tupper explicitly suggested expander hypergraphs as lower-bound sources, and Jozefiak–Shepherd ties the embedding question to hypergraph sparsest cut. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1712.01960)) Engine B — evolutionary/SAT/ILP construction search. Genome = rational δ values on subsets or a weighted hypergraph H inducing a hypergraph-Steiner diversity. Fitness = LP-minimal distortion c plus penalties for symmetry/triviality and for being in known easy classes. Mutations: add/remove hyperedges, change weights, lift from n to n+1, symmetrize/desymmetrize, or optimize δ directly by projected LP. For exactness, after floating search, rationalize δ and rerun exact LP; emit MPS/JSON plus dual certificates. For upper-bound certificates for a fixed n, use column generation over splits and a second-level robust LP over the diversity cone; this is harder and probably only realistic for n≤5 or highly symmetric subclasses in a week.

**Problem:** Let g(n) be the minimal size of A ⊆ {0,...,n} such that A+A contains every integer 0,...,n. Estimate g(n). In particular, is g(n) ~ 2√n? Current best bounds: (2.181...+o(1))n ≤ g(n)^2 ≤ (3.458...+o(1))n. **Win condition:** Provide an explicit finite set A ⊆ {0,...,n} (with reasonably large n, e.g. n up to several million or a mathematically meaningful asymptotic template) with |A| = k and a rigorous certificate (computer-verifiable) that A+A contains {0,...,n} and for which k^2 ≤ c n improves the current best constant (i.e. produces a smaller upper constant than 3.458...), OR produce an explicit provable lower-bound improvement (raising the constant >2.181...), or a Lean formalization that verifies any of the above certificates.

**Closest prior / novelty check:** Not killed as already solved. The closest prior is Kohonen 2017: a generalized Mrose construction gives additive 2-bases of size k=42t+7 and range n≥510t^2, hence liminf n(k)/k^2 ≥ 85/294 > 0.2891, equivalently an asymptotic upper constant k^2/n ≤ 294/85 = 3.458823... for g(n)^2/n. This is exactly the advertised upper-side record. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.04770)) The lower-side record in the user’s normalization is Yu 2015, a Fourier-series lower bound for the cardinality of finite h-bases; the Erdős Problems page records the current interval as (2.181...+o(1))n ≤ g(n)^2 ≤ (3.458...+o(1))n and marks the problem OPEN, with no comments claiming partial/complete solution. ([sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022314X15001535)) Beware normalization: much of the literature uses σ = limsup N/k(N)^2 or n(k)/k^2, so “upper bound for σ” corresponds to a lower bound on g(n)^2/n; “lower bound for σ” corresponds to an upper construction for g(n)^2/n. ([researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/243066923_Upper_bounds_for_finite_additive_2-bases)) Exact small-k/postage-stamp data are far from asymptotic: OEIS A001212 gives best 2-stamp ranges only through 24 denominations, and A066063 is the exact g(n) sequence for small n, so these do not close the asymptotic gap. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.04770)) An improvement in this same template would be a finite, checkable certificate with m/ell^2 > 510/42^2 = 85/294, where ell is the number of segments and m is the number of consecutive squares covered. That would immediately beat 3.458823... asymptotically after scaling t. However, the literature around postage-stamp/additive bases is old and fragmented; Nathanson’s 2026 survey explicitly warns of extensive forgotten German literature on n_h(k), including Rohrbach, Hofmeister, Mrose, Mossige, Kohonen, Yu, etc., so an “Erdősgate” risk remains for any claimed rediscovery. ([researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/405317643_Problems_in_additive_number_theory_VII_The_structure_of_additive_h-bases_for_n)) A lone finite A⊂[0,n] with k^2/n<3.458 is not meaningful by itself because many small exact postage-stamp examples already have such ratios; to beat the asymptotic record, the finite certificate must parameterize/scalably tile, or prove a repeatable construction. ([oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A001212?utm_source=openai))

No,” and the page/forum show no claimed partial solution. ([erdosproblems.com](https://www.erdosproblems.com/791?utm_source=openai)) However, LLM/evolutionary saturation is nonzero: AlphaEvolve’s public 2025/26 math repository contains 67 tested problems with prompts/verification code, and the accompanying paper emphasizes construction-search problems across combinatorics/number theory. ([github.com](https://github.com/google-deepmind/alphaevolve_repository_of_problems)) One listed adjacent problem is “Difference bases,” also interval coverage by pairwise differences, and it was suggested by an AI literature-search pipeline; this is not the same as finite additive 2-bases but is close enough that an AlphaEvolve-style search could already be naturally aimed here. ([sciencestack.ai](https://www.sciencestack.ai/paper/2511.02864?utm_source=openai)) GPT-Erdős exists as a public Erdős-problem tracker, but search hits did not reveal #791-specific work. ([ocf.berkeley.edu](https://www.ocf.berkeley.edu/~neel/erdos.html?utm_source=openai))

**Attack sketch:** Engine A, most credible: formalize Kohonen’s generalized-Mrose reduction and search for a better placement certificate. Represent each segment copy as type V/H/S placed at integer square coordinate p. Precompute which square intervals are covered by each pair type: V+H, V+S, H+S give full t×t squares/parallelograms up to O(t) boundary effects, while same-type sums are mostly negligible asymptotically. Then the objective is: choose finite I,J,K with ell total placements maximizing the initial covered run [0,m-1] in the union of I+J, I+K, J+K plus the known parallelogram-to-square implications. A certificate is just lists I,J,K plus interval-containment decompositions, like Kohonen’s proof split into subintervals. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.04770)) Run SAT/CP-SAT or MILP with Boolean variables x_{type,p}; impose coverage for all r<m by at least one valid pair; symmetry-break translation and first placements; maximize m/ell^2 over ell perhaps 20–100. If m/ell^2 exceeds 85/294 even by 1e-4, generate a short proof script: for each interval block [a,b], exhibit arithmetic progressions P⊂I and Q⊂J/K with [a,b]⊂P+Q, plus the segment-sum lemma. This is a realistic one-week target because the verifier is easy and the search space is finite, but finding an actual improvement may require luck. Engine B: direct evolutionary/SAT search for large A at n up to millions is less publishable unless it reveals a scalable pattern; use it mainly as pattern discovery, not as the final claim. Lower-bound improvement over Yu is RED-for-one-week: it likely requires new Fourier/test-function inequalities and rigorous numerical optimization, not just finite computation. Lean target: Lean-checking “given A covers [0,n]” is straightforward but not mathematically novel unless tied to a new scalable certificate; it may be useful as a reproducibility add-on, not as a standalone result.


## 3. [AMBER] erdos:653  (composite 4.5357, confidence medium)
**Source:** erdos · **Field:** ["combinatorics", "discrete-geometry", "number-theory"] · **Flags:** quantitative_extension,construction_target,default_method_stalled · [https://www.erdosproblems.com/653](https://www.erdosproblems.com/653)

**Problem:** For n points x_1,...,x_n in R^2 define R(x_i) = number of distinct distances from x_i to the other points. Order R(x_1) <= ... <= R(x_n). Let g(n) be the maximum possible number of distinct values appearing among the R(x_i). Known: g(n) > 7/10 n (Csizmadia) and g(n) < n - c n^{2/3}. Question: Is g(n) >= (1-o(1)) n? We seek modest, self-certifying progress (e.g. an explicit construction/family or finite certificate improving the constant).

**Win condition:** Produce an explicit family (parameterized construction) of n-point planar sets together with a proof (and for small n, machine-checkable certificates) that the number of distinct R(x_i) is >= c n for some c > 0.70 (i.e. strictly improving Csizmadia), or alternatively exhibit for a sequence of concrete n an explicit point set and a checked certificate (distance multiset counts) achieving a better fraction; all data and proof-checkable certificates must be provided (coordinates rational or algebraic, or exact symbolic description) so anyone can verify the claim.

**Closest prior / novelty check:** Closest exact prior is Csizmadia–Ismailescu, “Maximum Number of Different Distance Counts,” Bolyai Society Mathematical Studies 6 / Intuitive Geometry, 1997, pp. 301–309: it defines the same “distance count” D_d(n) and proves the planar lower bound D_2(n) ≥ about 0.7n, with higher-dimensional analogues; ResearchGate/MTMT list the paper and abstract, and ErdősProblems #653 records the current known bounds as g(n)>7n/10 and g(n)<n−c n^{2/3}. Erdős–Fishburn’s “Distinct distances in finite planar sets,” Discrete Math. 175 (1997), 97–132, is the earlier exact-small-n / vector-of-distance-counts paper, but only gives the weaker historical lower bound and small-n classifications. ([erdosproblems.com](https://www.erdosproblems.com/653))

**Residual (amber) risk:** I did not find a later paper closing the 0.70-to-1 asymptotic gap. The ErdősProblems page is current-looking and explicitly says OPEN, no partial/complete solution comments, and formalised statement = yes, but it also warns that site status is not a substitute for literature search. The “Erdősgate” risk here is moderate rather than fatal: the exact phrase “Maximum Number of Different Distance Counts” has few citations, so the literature is sparse and old; however, related distance/equidistance papers are numerous, and small finite wins are likely already known or not publishable. Beware that Csizmadia–Ismailescu also reports D_2(n)=n−1 for n≤5, so any finite certificate with a high ratio at tiny n is not novel. ([erdosproblems.com](https://www.erdosproblems.com/653))

**LLM-saturation:** No public evidence found that #653 itself has been solved or specifically attacked by OpenAI/DeepMind/AlphaEvolve. But the surrounding Erdős discrete-geometry space is now highly AI-saturated: OpenAI announced in May 2026 that an internal general-purpose model resolved the adjacent planar unit-distance conjecture while evaluating a collection of Erdős problems, with a human-verified companion note; DeepMind/Google public infrastructure includes formal-conjectures Erdős-problem issues and Aletheia/superhuman Erdős geometry outputs, though my searches did not surface #653 specifically. **Attack sketch:** Engine A, paper-first: obtain and dissect Csizmadia–Ismailescu’s 9-page construction; rewrite it as a parametric “gadgets + generic perturbation” lemma where each gadget contributes prescribed losses L_i=(n−1)−R(x_i). Try to replace any 10-block/7-good pattern by a 10-block/8-good or 20-block/15-good pattern. The likely route is not incidence theory but controlled centered-isosceles equalities: make many points have distinct numbers of forced duplicate radii while proving all unintended distance equalities are avoided by algebraic/generic choices. Engine B, search-first: encode candidate configurations as low-complexity templates—points on 2–4 lines/circles, translated small polygons, or integer/algebraic grids with symbolic parameters—and optimize the vector of exact distance-counts. Use SAT/SMT only for equality-pattern constraints; then solve realizability over Q or number fields and certify by exact squared-distance comparisons. Output should include coordinates plus a verifier listing, for every point, the partition of other points by equal squared distance. Realism: a finite record for moderate n is plausible in a week; a publishable asymptotic c>0.70 family is risky unless Csizmadia’s proof has an obvious slack/gadget substitution. Do not spend the week on generic random point sets: they give R(x)=n−1 for every x and hence g=1.


## 4. [AMBER] erdos:934  (composite 4.5, confidence medium)

**Problem:** For fixed integers t >=1 and degree bound d, h_t(d) is the least number of edges such that any graph with that many edges and maximum degree <= d necessarily contains two edges whose shortest path between them has length >= t. The task is to estimate h_t(d) (upper/lower bounds, exact values for small parameters, or constructions meeting conjectured asymptotics).

**Win condition:** Produce an explicit, checkable certificate: (a) a family of graphs (adjacency lists or constructive rule) for infinitely many d (or for concrete moderately large d) demonstrating a lower bound h_t(d) >= C_t d^t with an improved constant C_t over the literature for some fixed t (e.g. t=3 or small t), or (b) an exact computation of h_t(d) for specific small d,t improving known values (with exhaustive-search certificate), or (c) an improved universal upper bound h_t(d) <= K d^t with explicit proof. The certificate must include code/verification routine that checks max degree, edge count, and the pairwise edge-distance property.

**Closest prior / novelty check:** Closest prior is Cambie--Cames van Batenburg--de Joannis de Verclos--Kang, “Maximizing/Maximising Line Subgraphs of Diameter at Most t,” SIAM J. Discrete Math. 36(2):939--950, 2022. They explicitly identify this as the Erdős--Nešetřil edge-version of the degree--diameter problem; equivalently, h_t(Δ)-1 is the maximum number of edges in max-degree-Δ graphs whose line graph has diameter at most t. Their main general result is h_t(Δ) <= (3/2)Δ^t + 1, and for C_{2t+1}-free graphs they get exact bounds for t in {1,2,3,4,6}; they also prove h_3(3)=23. No”), and I found no direct hit for “erdos:934” / “Erdős Problem 934” in AI-solution chatter.
